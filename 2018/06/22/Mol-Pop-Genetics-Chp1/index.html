BASIC SEQUENCE TERMINOLOGY

allele: individual nucleotides (or amino acids) when they differ at a single position in the alignment. The set of alleles found on a single DNA strand is referred to as a haplotype.

Sequences in an alignment can be used to represent chromosomes from the natural population.

Mutation can be used for either all or rare polymorphism depending on the context. Here, mutation means the variation-generating process and the new mutations this process generates.

Substitution mean only those DNA differences observed between species, as distinct from variation within species.

MODELS OF EVOLUTIONARY PROCESSES

Models of populations

In natural populations, genetic drift refers to changes in allele frequencies in the new generation due to random sampling of chromsomes in the last generation. It is different from natural selection because, during genetic drift, the frequency of the allele is not the conquence of whether this allele can impact the fitness of the individuals by carrying the allele.

Writght-Fisher model (Fisher 1930b; Wright 1930) describes a population with constant size (N) of diploid hermaphrodites within a random mating system, and no individuals survive into the next generation (Which might lead to bias in our research).

Question: why there is only 0.5N mitochondrial or chloroplast genomes in population?

When the population is under only genetic drift, the change of allelic frequence is actually a sampling with a binomial distribution. Hence, the mean and variance of $p$ in the next generation will be $E(p_{t+1})=p_t$ and $Var(p_{t+1}) = p_tq_t/2N$.

Compared Writght-Fisher model, Moran model (Moran 1958) allows individuals of different ages and overlaps between different generations.
